# Keyword Finder
## Strategic keyword research and scoring for
## paid search and SEO — for any company,
## any campaign

---

## What This Skill Does
Generates a prioritized, scored keyword list
for paid search and SEO campaigns. Combines
company-specific buyer intelligence with
strategic competitive analysis to surface
both obvious and unexpected terms worth
targeting. Produces a grouped strategic view
and a ranked master list in one output file.
Saves to outputs/ only after confirmation.

---

## Step 1 — Read Before Doing Anything

Before asking a single question or researching
a single keyword, read ALL of these files.
Do not skip any of them.

### Required context files

context/icp.md
Pay close attention to:
- The Language They Actually Use section —
  this is your most valuable keyword source.
  Operational vocabulary, pain and frustration
  language, and strategic vocabulary are all
  potential high-intent search terms that
  generic keyword tools would never surface
- What Triggers Them to Start Looking —
  these are active search moments. Buyers
  searching during these triggers are the
  highest-intent audience you can capture
- Pain points by segment — quantified pain
  creates specific search language. Someone
  dealing with a polluted TM searches very
  differently than someone evaluating a
  new TMS for the first time
- Buyer personas and job titles — these
  inform which terms different personas
  are likely to search vs which they would
  never use

context/brand_voice.md
Pay close attention to:
- Words and phrases the company uses
  frequently — these are owned vocabulary
  that should anchor keyword clusters
- Words and phrases to avoid — if the
  company avoids certain language in copy,
  keywords built around that language will
  create a disconnect between ad and landing
  page. Flag these.

context/competitors.md
Pay close attention to:
- Where competitors are weak — these are
  keyword gaps worth targeting aggressively
- Messaging angles competitors already own —
  terms heavily associated with competitor
  positioning need competitive context
  notes so the human can decide whether
  to compete or avoid
- Competitor names — branded competitor
  keywords (e.g. "[competitor] alternative")
  are a separate category with their own
  strategic logic. Include them but flag
  them clearly as competitor displacement
  terms

context/market.md
Pay close attention to:
- Emerging trends and vocabulary — new
  market language often has low competition
  and high relevance. Early movers on
  trend-driven keywords can own them
  before competition catches up
- Market size and growth areas — high
  growth segments generate new search
  behavior worth capturing early

### Optional context files
Check for these and use if found.
If not found, proceed without them:

context/proof_points.md
If found: named customer results and
specific metrics can inform keyword
angles — buyers searching for proof
of ROI use different terms than buyers
searching for product features.

outputs/campaign_writer_* (any recent files)
If found: scan for terms and angles
already being used in copy. Keywords
should align with and support active
campaign messaging where possible.

context/deep/trigger_events.md
If found: surface trigger-driven keyword
opportunities — buyers searching during a
trigger event are the highest-intent audience
you can capture. If not found, proceed
without it.

context/deep/market_gaps.md
If found: surface gap-driven keyword
opportunities — unclaimed positioning angles
often have low competition and high strategic
value. If not found, proceed without it.

## Step 4 — Scoring Model

Score every keyword against these four
criteria. Show the score for each category
individually AND the total. Never show
only a total score.

### ICP vocabulary match (0-25 points)
How closely does this term match the exact
language buyers use in icp.md?

25 points: Exact phrase from icp.md that
buyers use to describe their own problem
(e.g. "vendor management tax",
"localization debt", "polluted TM")

15-20 points: Close variation of ICP
language or operational vocabulary from
the skill file (e.g. "translation memory
management", "TMS vendor evaluation")

5-10 points: Relevant category term buyers
would recognize but wouldn't naturally
use themselves (e.g. "enterprise
translation software")

0-4 points: Generic term with weak ICP
specificity (e.g. "translation services",
"language solutions")

### Intent and funnel fit (0-25 points)
Is the search intent clear and does it
map to a stage where paid capture makes
sense?

25 points: High intent, specific to a
buying moment or active evaluation
(e.g. "TMS implementation cost",
"Smartling alternative enterprise")

15-20 points: Clear intent, mid-funnel
evaluation behavior (e.g. "best
enterprise TMS", "localization platform
comparison")

5-10 points: Informational intent,
awareness stage, lower conversion
probability (e.g. "what is localization",
"translation management explained")

0-4 points: Ambiguous intent — unclear
whether searcher is a buyer, a student,
or a translator looking for work

### Winnability (0-25 points)
Based on competitor landscape in
competitors.md — is this realistically
ownable or completely dominated?

25 points: Clear gap — competitors are
weak or absent here, low competition
likely, high opportunity

15-20 points: Competitive but not
dominated — worth testing with strong
copy and a differentiated landing page

5-10 points: Heavily contested — major
competitors own this term, requires
significant budget to compete

0-4 points: Dominated by a single
competitor who owns this positioning —
show competitive context so human
can decide

### Strategic value (0-25 points)
Does owning this term advance a
positioning angle from competitors.md
that this company can uniquely own?

25 points: Directly supports a unique
positioning angle no competitor owns
(e.g. "air-gapped translation platform"
for government — unique to this company)

15-20 points: Supports a differentiated
angle even if not completely unique
(e.g. "translation AI that learns
brand voice" — owned angle even if
others claim adjacent territory)

5-10 points: Relevant but generic —
supports the category without advancing
unique positioning

0-4 points: Commodity term with no
differentiation opportunity

### Score thresholds
80-100: Priority targets — lead with
these in campaign build
60-79: Worth testing — include in
initial campaigns, monitor performance
40-59: Situational — useful for specific
campaigns or content, not core paid spend
Below 40: Deprioritize — flag with note
on why it scored low

## Step 5 — Produce The Output

Structure the full keyword output in
three sections:

### Section 1 — Strategic keyword groups

Organize all keywords into these groups.
Not every group will apply to every run —
only include groups that have keywords:

**Pain and frustration keywords**
Terms buyers use when describing their
own problem. Highest ICP vocabulary match.
Often the most unexpected terms that
generic tools miss entirely.

**Evaluation keywords**
Terms buyers use when actively evaluating
solutions in this category. Mid to bottom
funnel. High commercial intent.

**Outcome and ROI keywords**
Terms buyers use when searching for
proof that solutions work. Often used
by economic buyers and executive sponsors
building a business case.

**Security and compliance keywords**
(Include only if relevant to segment)
Terms used by regulated industries,
government, or enterprise security
teams. Often low competition, high
specificity, extremely high intent
for the right buyer.

**Trend and emerging vocabulary keywords**
Terms from market.md that are growing
in search behavior. Lower competition
now, worth owning before competitors
catch up.

**Competitor displacement keywords**
(Only if requested in answers)
"[Competitor] alternative", "switch
from [competitor]", "vs [competitor]"
style terms. Flag each with the
competitive context from competitors.md
so the human can make an informed
bidding decision.

**Unexpected buyer vocabulary keywords**
Terms surfaced from the pain and
frustration language section of icp.md
that most keyword tools would never
generate — because they require knowing
how this specific buyer describes their
own problem in their own words.
This group is often the highest value
and least competitive.

## Rules This Skill Must Always Follow

Never show only a total score — always
show the breakdown by all four categories.

Never recommend avoiding a keyword
solely because a competitor uses it —
show competitive context and let the
human decide.

Always include an unexpected vocabulary
group — if the icp.md has rich buyer
language, there will always be terms
worth surfacing that generic tools miss.

Always include negative keyword notes —
wasted spend from wrong-intent clicks
is as important as identifying the
right keywords.

Never save without explicit confirmation.

Always read all four required context
files before producing any keywords —
never skip this step.

If any required context file is missing,
stop and say so before proceeding.

Follow the file naming convention in CLAUDE.md
for all output files.

The ranked master list must include
every keyword that appeared in the
groups — no keywords in groups missing
from the master list.
